HOW AFFILIATES WORK

Affiliate programs are a great way to make money on the internet.  The basic idea is that you'll make money by driving traffic to other websites and then getting a commission on whatever they do there (buy something, sign up for more information, click on an ad, etc.).

One very effective way of doing this is to establish a website around a theme, fill it with keyword-rich content about that theme, and populate it with links to good, relevant affiliate products.  As you promote this site, you'll draw visitors.  As the visitors see your site and your content, many will click away to the affiliate sites, and make purchases, bringing you commission money.

There are a lot of advantages to running a business this way:

  • Since all of the customers make their purchases away from your website, you don't have to establish any kind of credit card processing system.  There's no merchant account, no money changing.  Since you're not taking the order, you don't have to even set up a business to get started.  In the long run, you might want to do that anyway, but at the start, it's not necessary.
  • Since the other website is handling the purchases, they also have to handle order fulfillment.  You don't have to process any orders, maintain any inventory, and do any shipping.  The other company will also handle all returns and complaints.
  • If you choose your affiliate program well, you can piggy-back on an established, proven business.  You don't have to compete with the big boys, since you're working with them!
  • Finally, you can focus on driving traffic instead of processing orders and dealing with suppliers.


Like all things, there are some disadvantages, too.

  • Since you're basically getting a simple referral fee, your share of the profit margins can be low.  You'll need to drive lots of traffic to make the same amount of money.
  • If a customer comes to your site, chooses to buy a product, and clicks away to make the purchase, it can be tricky to get them to come back and buy something else.  It's not possible to have multiple products in a shopping cart.
  • In many ways, you're at the mercy of your affiliate provider.  You can't set the retail price of the products, for example.  And if a company discontinues a critical product line, you have no say.


TANGIBLE V. ELECTRONIC PRODUCTS

There are a couple of different approaches to affiliate products.  One approach is a retailer that sells tangible products.  These are items that, when purchased, get packaged into a box or envelope and shipped to the customer.  This is like buying a book at Amazon.com.

Another approach is electronic, or downloadable, products.  A good example of this kind of product is an eBook from ClickBank (we'll talk about them soon).  These products don't have a tangible, touchable form.  Instead, their data that can be delivered via internet download.  The customer makes the purchase, and, as soon as his or her credit card is approved, is sent an email with download instructions.  He or she then clicks through the email to download the eBook, and can read it immediately on his or her screen, or even print it out for casual reading later.

Because tangible products have real manufacturing, storing, and shipping costs, and downloadable products don't, must tangible products will have a much narrower profit margin.  We'll be showing you how to find and research both kinds of products for your website, but we recommend that the site initially focus on downloadable information products.

The Number Of Competing Pages

The first indicator you'll look at is the number of sites that you'll be competing with for top rankings in Google's Index.  The number of competing pages is sometimes referred to as the SEOC or the Search Engine Optimization Competition.  Now realistically you'll only be competing with the first page of results and that's why we'll be looking at other factors such as PageRank and Backlinks but we want to look at the number of competitors because this is often an indicator of how long it will take to rank on Google's results. 

To find the number of competing pages you'll go to Google.com, enter in one of your phrases- for example "scrapbooking", then click SEARCH.  Once the results have loaded you'll look at the top of the search results, just under the search box- at the text that reads similar to:  "About 17,000,000 results (0.30 seconds)".  Your SEOC (Number of Competing Pages) will be the number you see there (17,000,000 in our example).

Keep in mind that Google updates their database many times throughout the month so this number can and most likely will change but because we're only using it as one of many factors in researching your niche, it's still very helpful. 

It's important to know that there is no strict rule as to how many competing pages are "good" or "bad" for your niche.  It is simply an indicator of competition.  The fewer competing pages there are, the faster you'll get to the first page using the strategies you'll be learning shortly.  The more competing pages there are, the more work it will take.  So if you're deciding between two niche ideas that meet the rest of our criteria and the first keyword has 1 million competing pages while the second only has 100,000- you should choose the second. 

That being said, we do suggest that your chosen niche have no more than 5,000,000 competing pages (for the main keyword you'll be trying to rank for).  More pages than that and getting to the first page with your new website will probably take too long.

Find PageRank of Top 5 Sites

The number of Competing Pages gave you an idea as to how much competition there is for your niche ideas... here we'll be looking at the quality of that competition. 

PageRank is a numerical score (from 0-10) given to each webpage by Google- with 10 being the highest possible PageRank score.  PageRank measures a webpage's importance on the web.  To Google, the most important webpages are the ones that are linked to the most from other important, trusted, relevant webpages.  Think of a link as a vote.  When one webpage links to another webpage it is casting a vote for the importance of that webpage.  The more votes (links) a webpage receives, the more important it will look in Google's eyes.

A higher PageRank score is earned when a page not only receives a lot of votes (links) but those votes come from webpages that also have a high PageRank score (a high level of importance).  The higher a webpage's PageRank, the more weight it's vote has when linking to other webpages.

Why is it important to know the PageRank of the highest ranking websites in your niche?

Because in any niche your goal is to outrank the competing websites and achieve the highest possible position in Google's search results, giving your site the most traffic.  The higher the PageRank (importance) of those competing websites the harder it will be to outrank them. 

You want to know how much work will be involved and how much time it will take to outrank those sites before entering a niche.  The higher their PR, the harder it will be to outrank them.  The lower their PR, the less work it will take.

Here, you'll focus on the PageRank of the websites in the Top 5 positions in Google's search results for your niche keywords.  If the PR of these sites is too high, it will be too tough to rank your site high enough to get the traffic you need.  In later steps, we will show you how to increase the PageRank of your own site but in this step we will be focusing on finding out the PageRank of the sites you'll be competing against.

Number of Backlinks

Next you'll look at the number of backlinks pointing to your competitor's websites. 

A backlink is simply a link from one site pointing back to another site (often referred to as simply a link or an inlink).  For example if "Website A" creates a link on it's page to "Website B", "Website B" has just received 1 backlink.  To outrank a website you need more "better quality" backlinks than it has.  Search engines work just like an election, whoever gets the most votes wins the election. 

The more votes (backlinks) your site receives the higher it's ranking will be.  As a website owner you want as many backlinks as you can find and the higher the PR of the site linking to you the more impact the backlink will have on your ranking.  We look at the number of backlinks that the top ranking websites have to get a general idea of how many backlinks your site will need to outrank them. 

There are a number of methods and tools that you can use to find the number of backlinks pointing to a website.  We'll be using Yahoo's backlink tool to find the number of backlinks because it offers the most in depth results.   Some tools you can use to access Yahoo's backlink results include:

Type of Listings in Google

Now that you've researched the most important criteria such as PageRank, Backlinks, etc... lastly you want to look at the types of sites that you'll be competing with. 

It's important to understand that there are certain types of sites that search engines will give preference to mainly because they find them more authoritative.  As you go through this research process, you will see several different types of search results appear in the Search Engine Results Pages including image results, shopping results, video results, double listings, and authority listings.

Sometimes it can get confusing seeing so many different search results, not to mention all the different types of URL's you might come across such as .com, .edu, .gov, .info etc..  The purpose of this step is to educate you on what these different results are and how it will effect you being able to outrank your competition.  First we'll address the types of URL's that could possibly be difficult to outrank. 


Types of URL's:

.GOV- These types of URL's are sites related to Government Agencies such as state's websites, tax commissions, board of health etc..  The reason these sites will be so difficult to outrank is the fact that they usually have thousands of backlinks and typically high PageRank.   In most cases that's because several sites reference them as authority sites on several different topics.  Also, since these sites are purely informational they usually get a higher PageRank than an ecommerce site will ever be able to get. 

.EDU- Sites that end in a .edu rather than a .com are sites that have to do with educational institutions.  These can be public or private schools, grade schools, or elementary.  The reason these sites can be difficult to outrank is the fact that like .GOV sites they are usually purely informational sites and usually if they are sites for public schools they are usually linked to by some of the strong .GOV sites giving them even more authority in the eyes of the search engines.

.INFO, .BIZ etc..- As far as outranking your competition, .gov and .edu URL's are the only one's that will give you any real trouble.  Other URL's that you'll see include .info, .biz and many others but these don't carry any extra weight in the search engine's eyes. 

As with all of the indicators that we've covered, none of them are the end all be all answer. So you'll also want to look at the other indicators such as PageRank, # of Backlinks, niche value etc.  Also keep in mind that if you are researching terms that a lot of .edu or .gov sites are appearing for, usually those terms are not "buyer" keywords. 

In addition to seeing several types of URL's as you do your research, you will also come across several different types of listings.  Google is always testing new types of listings. Sometimes these will appear, sometimes they will not, and sometimes they will appear in different locations on the page.  Take the time to become familiar with the different types of listings you'll find in the search results. 

Types of Listings:

  • Authority Listings- Authority listings are sites that Google feels are an authority in a given niche or on a given topic.  You can usually tell authority listings because they not only have links to their main page but also several links to other pages on their site.  This type of listing is not to be confused with dual listings that will also appear.
  • Dual Listings- Dual or Indented listings occur when one website has multiple URL's that rank in the top 10.  Rather than having two separate listings, Google simply moves the two listings together and indents the lower ranking page under the other.  For example if you had a page that ranked in the first position and another page that ranked in the 9th position, rather than having two listings your second page would appear as an indented listing underneath your #1 ranking page.
  • Sponsored Links- Whenever you do a search there are ads that appear at the top and on the right hand side of the search results, those ads are called sponsored links.  Sponsored links appear there because advertisers are paying for those ads to appear, you don't need to worry about those for now, we will focus on sponsored listings at a later point.
  • Image Results- Sometimes image results will appear in the search results, usually they will only appear when phrases are typed in that have lower commercial intent.  Getting listed in the Google image search shouldn't be a huge priority for you because people looking for images aren't usually buyers.
  • Video Results- Google pulls it's videos results from the top listings in Youtube, Metacafe, Howcast.com etc.. Getting your videos listed in the video results section will allow you to receive a huge amount of traffic but can take some time.  In later sections we will cover how to get your videos listed in the video results but for now you don't need to worry about them effecting your niche research.
  • News Results- News results typically only appear when you search for something newsworthy such as "The Oscars" "The Superbowl" etc.. so you most likely won't need to compete or worry about getting listed in the news results.
  • Twitter Results- Lately Google has been experimenting with importing a Twitter feed into their search results.  Like the news results this usually only appears if you are searching for a hot topic or something newsworthy that has a lot of people talking about it.
  • Shopping Results- Google imports some of their Google Products results into the search results, most of them come from people that have imported their product lists directly into the Google Base Database but some of them come from feeds coming from eBay Stores and other sites that Google has partnered with.  We will cover how to get listed at the top of the Google Products results in later sections.  This is one of the most sporadic listings because Google is always testing it's positions, don't be surprised if you see this listed on the top, bottom or side of the organic search results.


With an ecommerce site you'll be able to utilize several of these types of listings to increase your ranking and your traffic, later in the marketing curriculum we'll show you how exactly to get listed in the different results.

You've been doing a lot of research and gathering a lot of hard data to determine which of your niche ideas will likely be the best.  There's one last research step that's a little less defined, but which is just as important, if not moreso.  That is the study of the competition and the current market out on the web itself.

This research begins at a search engine, like Google.com or Yahoo.com.  Do a search for keywords that relate to the remaining products you have in your list.  Begin visiting the sites that appear. Some of the sites you see will be commercial, selling products, and some will be purely informational.  That's OK; you can learn from all of them, even from the bad ones! Look them over and ask yourself these questions:

  • What are some of the first elements of the website that I notice?  What grabs my eye first?  What elements are at the top of the page?
  • What products are they selling?  Which of those products are they featuring?
  • What content, or information, are they sharing?  Is that content easy to find?
  • As you click into the site, and the catalog, is it easy to navigate?
  • Can you easily find a way to contact them, if you had a question?
  • Would you buy from them?  What elements of the website inspire or undermine your confidence in them?
  • What is the Google page rank of the site?  The Google page rank is a way of measuring the overall effectiveness of the site, from Google's point of view.  If your website has a good page rank, then it will usually list higher in the search results at Google.  


These will give you some ideas of things that your competition is doing that both works and doesn't work.  You'll be able to mimic the good ideas and avoid the mistakes.

A second part of the research is to find out what's being said in your industry.  A great way to discover "the buzz" about a topic is to read some blogs.  Blogs are specialized websites where people can share their thoughts, opinions, and ideas.  Again, return to the search engine and search for these same keywords, but include the word "blog" this time.  For example, instead of typing "Fishing," type "Fishing Blog."

Click into these blog websites and begin reading.  Not only will you learn a lot about what's being said about your niche, you'll also learn a lot about blogging and how it works.  This will come in handy later on, when you're promoting your website.

GOOD KEYWORDS

A strong keyword is a word (or 2-3 word phrase) that has good demand (lots of people are searching for it), and not too much competition (not many other websites are using it).  Does this sound familiar?  When you were doing your initial niche research, you were essentially checking on the strength of keywords that relate to your potential product lines.  In fact, much of the research you did then will apply to this task as well.

For the home page, identify three keywords that meet these criteria:
  • Each one should be relevant to the overall content of your website.
  • At least one of the keywords should have demand of at least 1,000 searches per month, and competition of not more than 2,500,000.
  • The other two keywords should have competition no higher than 2,500,000, and the demand should be as high as possible.
  • No more than two of them should contain the same word.  For example, a good collection of three might include: "Baby Strollers," "Jogging Strollers,"  and "Baby Carriage."  Two of them contain "Baby,"  and two of them contain "Strollers,"  but those words are not found in all three. It will become difficult to maintain a good balance of keywords in your text if you violate this guideline.



Also, you might notice that in your research, there is a difference in the demand numbers between the singular and plural versions of a word.  Singular and plurals are actually different keywords and should be researched and used uniquely.


Ultimately, you'll build subpages and product category pages in your site.  Even though we'll be going over these things in detail later, they should also have good, strong keywords as well.  For each subpage in your site, identify 2-3 keywords that meet these criteria:
  • Each one should be relevant to the content of that particular page.  In other words, if you have a page in your camping site that's all about backpacking, the keywords for that page should also relate to backpacks and backpacking.
  • Meeting the demand and competition criteria for the subpage keywords is not as critical as it is for the main page keywords.  Still, they should be as strong as possible.
  • Again, as above, no more than two of them should contain the same word.

KEYWORDS IN YOUR TEXT

Next to your website's domain name and title, the most important place for a search engine to find keyword matches is within the visible text of your website.

You want to have a lot of repetitions of your keywords within the page, but not too much.  Your goal is to have your strongest keywords (the ones included in your meta tags) in the text of your site to a 2%- 5% density.  We use a tool called SEOQuake to check the density. You can also check your density percentages manually.  If you just do a word count of the total words you have written for your home page, you will want to use the following guidelines:
  • 1 word phrase: 2-5 repetitions per 100 words - Maximum of 25 repetitions per 500 words
  • 2 word phrase: 1-3 repetitions per 100 words - Maximum of 12 repetitions per 500 words
  • 3 word phrase: 1-2 repetitions per 100 words - Maximum of 8 repetitions per 500 words
  • 4 word phrase: 1-2 repetition per 100 words - Maximum of 6 repetitions per 500 words


It's also important not to have any words (other than common ones like "the" or "and," etc.)  that have more than a 5% saturation.  That can actually reduce your search engine effectiveness.

WEBSITE META AND OTHER TAGS

The information in the meta tags doesn't appear on the site itself, but they function "under the hood," so to speak.  The three meta tags that are most used in internet marketing are the "Title,"  "Description,"  and "Keywords" tags. It is important to do the keyword research before writing description and keywords so you know what words to target for your specific product or business service.

Title tags tell the search engine the main focus of what the webpage is about and the main focus of the related content. While it's technically not a true meta tag, it shares the same function, so it's often grouped with them when people are speaking of it. Your title should be no longer than 66 characters, and include at least one of your strongest keywords.

Keyword tags tell the search engine what keywords are important for the specific page.  In recent years, the value of keyword matches in the meta keywords tag has faded, to the point that some search engines ignore this space altogether.  It does, however, still serve as a good reference point, and there are some search engines that still utilize it.

THE VALUE OF LINKING

Essentially, all of marketing on the internet comes down to one thing:  inbound links.  These are links that people can click on to come to your website from some other place, like another site, a directory, an article, a search engine, or even an email.  There are three ways that links are important:

  • Direct clicking - If a visitor to another website sees that there's a link to your site, there's an opportunity for them to click through to you.  It then stands to reason that the more links you have out on the net pointing to you, the more traffic they'll send.  An additional benefit is that this traffic gets directly to your site, bypassing the search engines completely.
  • Search engine ranking - Search engines, especially Google, check to see how many other sites and other pages are linking to yours.  They equate this with importance and popularity, and if you have a lot of links, you'll get ranked higher.  Getting lots of quality links is called "off-site SEO."
  • Search engine indexing - Search engines have programs (called, "spiders," "crawlers," and "robots") that regularly scour the internet, looking for more sites to add to their listings, and looking to update the listings of the sites they already have.  The more and better links you have coming to your site, the faster you'll get added to the search engines, and the more often your site will be updated in their listings.

To be effective, content needs to be informative, useful, and not just advertising for a product or service.  The internet is already full of advertising copy, and most people are learning to ignore it.  If something on the web teaches them how to do something, or if they learn from it, then they'll look deeper to find products.

  • Tags - Also known as "labels," these are clusters of keywords used to help visitors and search engines find relevant posts.
  • RSS - A method readers use to "subscribe" to a blog, so they can be alerted whenever new content is added.

In order to understand this next step, we need to understand some important things about internet website promotion and search engine optimization.

The Value of Linking

Essentially, all marketing on the internet comes down to one thing:  inbound links.  These are links that people can click on to come to your website from some other place, like another site, a directory, an article, a search engine, or even an email.  There are three ways that links are important:


  • Direct clicking - If a visitor to another website sees that there's a link to your site, there's an opportunity for them to click through to you.  It then stands to reason that the more links you have out on the net pointing to you, the more traffic they'll send.  An additional benefit is that this traffic gets directly to your site, bypassing the search engines completely.
  • Search engine ranking - Search engines, especially Google, check to see how many other sites and other pages are linking to yours.  They equate this with importance and popularity, and if you have a lot of links, you'll get ranked higher.  Getting lots of quality links is called "off-site SEO."
  • Search engine indexing - Search engines have programs (called "spiders," "crawlers," and "robots") that regularly scour the internet, looking for more sites to add to their listings, and looking to update the listings of the sites they already have.  The more and better links you have coming to your site, the faster the spiders will find you, and the faster you'll get added to the search engines.  Your site will also be updated in their listings more often.


The Value of Content

Studies show that most people, when they're not checking their emails or paying their bills online, are usually looking for information first and shopping second.  That means that if you have good, usable information at your site, or set up in peripheral sites with links to your site, there will be more ways for people to find you and they'll be more interested in clicking through to your site.  In addition, search engines love to index content, because it gives them something of substance with good keyword saturation to index.

To be effective, content needs to be informative, useful and not just advertising for a product or service.  The internet is already full of advertising copy, and most people are learning to ignore it.  If something on the web teaches them how to do something, or if they learn from it, then they'll look deeper to find products.

As you move forward with the steps that follow, they'll all be focused on one of those two things, or possibly both: links and content.

A third approach to blogging keywords involves making more money by including keywords that draw in more effective AdSense ads from Google.  Here's how it works:


  • When someone (a visitor) clicks on one of your Google AdSense ads, you get a small percentage of the pay-per-click amount that that particular advertiser paid for that click.
  • So, if someone clicks on an ad that cost $1.00 per click, you'll make more than if they clicked on an ad that had a $0.25 bid.
  • Google assigns ads to your blog based on the content.  In other words, each time you have a visitor at your site, Google scans the content of your blog, picks out some keywords, and matches ads where the bidder chose those particular keywords.
  • So, if your site is filled with keywords that have higher-priced bidding, you'll have more valuable ads on your site, and you'll make more money when people click.


First, you'll need to learn to use a new research tool:  The Google AdWords Keyword Tool.  Launch this tool, and do some checking into the same keywords you researched at the beginning.  You're not looking for keywords to rank well on, however, so you don't need to worry about the Global Demand column or the advertiser competition bars.  In this research you'll look for something else.

Go to the "Choose columns to display" dropdown menu and select "Show estimated avg CPC."  That stands for "cost per click".  You'll see a new column appear in the table for that information, and when you click on the header of that column, it will sort the table by the values in that column.  Click until you have the most expensive words at the top of the list.

Now you can start including these words (that are still, obviously, relevant to your niche topic) into your blog posts.  This will generate ads containing these more expensive keywords, and you'll make more money per click.  You don't need to research the competition on these keywords, since you're not using them with the intention of improving your ranking.  These keywords are in your blog solely for the purpose of increasing your revenue.
